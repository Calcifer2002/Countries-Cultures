Traditions of Nigeria

The cuisine of Nigeria is deeply rooted in the ancient times when food
was related to art, traditions, and culture. Nigerian cuisine is known for being spicy and
ypically grain-based, and most dishes are made from starch. Nigerian cuisine comprises hundreds
of ethnic groups that comprise Nigeria. Nigerian dishes are lavish and colorful, it consists of
spices and herbs to create heavily flavored sauces. Each region in Nigeria has its own regional
favorite that is dependent on customs, tradition, and religion. The different food available also
depends on the season called the “hungry season”. A large part of Nigeria lies in the tropical part,
where fruits and vegetables are available. Some of the popular fruits are oranges, melons, grapefruits,
limes, and mangoes. People in the northern parts of Nigeria who are mainly Muslims have diets that consists
of beans, sorghum, and brown rice. The Hausa tribe eats meat in the form of tsere or suya. The Yoruba tribe,
in central Nigeria, tend to eat meat stews and mashed yams or mashed cassava. The West of the country prepares
meals using cassava flour and has specific dishes consisting of frozen fried snails, or dried shrimp.

Because of the lack of refrigerators in the past, the method of preparation
in Nigerian cuisine relates to salting the meat after sacrificing the animals or else the meat would go
bad. Special condiments are used to prepare Nigerian cuisine for example, black-eyed peas, ukazi leaves,
bitterleaf and chilli peppers to name a few. A lot of wooden dishes are used for preparing and serving
Nigerian cuisine. Deep serving dishes are required for the traditional Nigerian soups and for the special
fish stews. Many recipes and preparations have been passed down from one generation to another.


Nigeria is one of the most religiously diverse countries in the world due to
its rapidly growing population. The belief system of Nigeria is heavily influenced
by the colonizers. Even though colonization brought Christianity to Nigeria, there
are still places in Nigeria that practice traditional religions. While the south of
Nigeria is dominated by Christianity, the north is predominantly filled with Islam.
Which was spread due to Fulani Jihadist war campaigns of Usman Dan Fodio especially
among the Hausa tribe before the British came. Every tribe in Nigeria has its own
traditional belief system. The three major tribes are Hausa, Igbo, and Yoruba. As
explained before, the Hausa people are mostly representatives of the Islam religion
they are presently the least influenced by traditional religious beliefs. During the
11th century, Islam was brought to Nigeria by traders from Guinea, Mali, and Borno to
the Hausa tribe who accepted and supported its spread. The major Hausa traditional religion
is called Maguzawa which was practiced in remote parts of Hausaland in pre-colonial Nigeria.
Maguzawa is the name for a subgroup of the Hausa people. People who practice Maguzawa
believe in three thousand spirits which are divided into two major groups. The Gona
(Farm Spirit) and the Daji (Bush spirits).

The six major spirits are:

  • Bagiro = the spirit that can devour souls
  • Manzo = this spirit is described as a dog that can torture souls
  • Babban Maza = meaning “great among men”
  • Waziri = the spirit that provides gifts to people
  • Mai’iali = this spirit is said to be the queen of the spirits and is portrayed by children wearing a large cloth
  • Sarki Aljah = the spirit is said to be the king of all spirits


Bori is another Hausa religion that has been practiced before colonization.
Spirit possession is the main feature, for example, they used spirits to heal illnesses by
performing exorcisms. Before colonization occurred the Igbo tribe in the South-Eastern part of
Nigeria believed in Odinani. They believed in one God (Chineke or Chukwu) who also had lots of
spirits and Odinani had a monotheistic attribute. The religion divided the world into Human and
Spirit worlds. Chineke is believed to be the creator of everything. It is taught that each person
had a guardian spirit which is called “Chi” and was given to the person by Chineke. A person’s Chi
was believed to follow them into the spirit world at death as death was believed to be a transit of
humans to the spirit world.
Chukwu was the supreme spirit but there were also lesser spirits, such as


  • Anyanwu = the god of the sun that controlled the weather
  • Amadioha = the god of the sky that controlled justice and lightning
  • Ana or Anada = the Earth goddess
  • Ikenga = the god of time and achievement who also had control over blacksmithing and farming
  • Ekwensu = the god of war that was mostly worshipped among warriors


The system of priests under the Odinani was divided into two types: Hereditary Priests -
were the servants of a particular god or spirit and the Seer - were the priests that were empowered with
justice functions, symbols of power, and almost limitless authority. The Igbo tribe also believed in
ancestral worship, they would communicate with their ancestors in the spirit world and made offerings
to their dead in ancestral shrines which were dedicated to ancestral worship. Finally, the Yoruba tribe
in the SouthWest part of Nigeria still practices their religion’s traditional beliefs of which the key element
is destiny “Ayanmo”. The Yoruba people believe in one Supreme God, who they call Olodumare/Eledumare, Olorun, and Olori.
They believe in lesser gods and spirits which have the ability to travel between Heaven and Earth. Yoruba’s also practice
ancestral worship by offering gifts and pouring libations to their dead.
