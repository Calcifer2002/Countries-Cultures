History of Australia

Approximately 50,000 to 60,000 years ago, the first inhabitants of Australia, the Aboriginal people, arrived from some unknown point in Asia. Capt. James Cook has been credited with discovering Australia, but the Portuguese were likely to have seen the country first, and the Dutch explored the coastal regions in 1640. A British fleet under Captain Arthur Phillip first entered Botany Bay in January 1788, bringing Europe's first settlement in Australia. A penal colony originally, by the 1830s, an increasing number of free settlers began settling there. It was abolished in 1852 for convicts to be transported to the eastern colonies and in 1868 for those in the western colonies.

Australia lies between the Pacific and Indian Oceans in the Southern Hemisphere, is the smallest continent on earth, and one of the biggest countries on the planet. The capital of Australia is Canberra, which is located in the southeast, between Sydney and Melbourne, two of the world's most prominent economic and cultural hubs. Australasia stretches nearly 2,500 miles east to west, extending from the Cape York Peninsula, 200 miles northeast, to Wilson's Promontory, in the south. Further south, Australia extends its jurisdiction along the southern tip of Tasmania for 310 miles and reaches Papua New Guinea's southern shores for another 310 miles. A member of the Coral Sea and Torres Strait, Papua New Guinea is located to the northeast of Australia. The Coral Sea Islands Territory lies to the southwest, within the Tasman Sea. Antarctica resides in south Antarctica. Australia has been called "the oldest continent," "the last of the lands," and "the last frontier" by its natives.
