History of Canada

For millennia, various groups of indigenous people inhabited the land now owned by Canada. The Atlantic coast was discovered and later settled by British and French expeditions at the end of the 15th century. In 1763, after the Seven Years' War, France ceded nearly all of its colonies in North America. Confederation formed Canada as a federal dominion of four provinces in 1867, with the union of three British colonies in North America. Legal dependence on the British Parliament ended only following the 1982 passage of the Canada Act. Canada is a federal federation consisting of 10 provinces and 3 territories. Located roughly in the northern two-fifths of North America, Canada is the second-largest country in the world (after Russia). Although Canada is one of the world's largest countries, it has one of the least populated populations.

Anna Brownell Jameson, an Irish writer who explored central Ontario in 1837 and exulted over the seemingly endless forest before him. She noted that this fact, along with the grandeur of the landscape, has played an influential part in the shaping of Canadian national identity. It was a fascinating experience to journey through the wilderness; to be enveloped by untamed wilderness; to explore the mysterious depths amid the tangle of dense foliage...the solitude with which we traveled mile after mile without being seen by another ." Although Canada has a relatively small population, it has become a model multicultural society, attracting immigrants from everywhere in the world. The Canadian economy is not only rich in natural resources but also intellectual capital and exports.
