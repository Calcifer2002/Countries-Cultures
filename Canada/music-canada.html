Music of Canada

Canadian music reflects the diversity of influences that have shaped the country. Throughout Canada's musical heritage, Native Americans, Irish, British, and French influences can be found. As a consequence of the proximity between the two countries, the music was also influenced by American culture. Samuel de Champlain, the explorer from France, arrived at Port Royal and Qu√©bec in 1605 and established the first permanent French settlements. Since then, Canada has produced composers, musicians, and ensembles. As a result of living in North America for thousands of years, the music of the Inuit and the Indians has been influenced. Despite their involvement in native music, European observers from the 17th century such as Marc LESCARBOT, Father Paul LE JEUNE, and Father Gabriel Sagard were as fascinated as unqualified to describe and analyze native music as they were by the exotic sounds and sights of native music-making. Music has been little affected by native music since the beginning of colonization in the 17th century. Song, dance, and religious chants were introduced by the original settlers, and successive waves of immigration built on that tradition.

As a result of the importation of printed music, of teachers, and of touring performances, followed later by recordings and broadcasting, the original expression has been suppressed or at least delayed. Throughout Canada's history, the music has paralleled the major European style periods such as the Baroque, Classical, Romantic, and Contemporary. It is impossible to achieve success in transplanting Old World patterns in a sparsely populated country with widely scattered settlements, however. Consequently, opera houses and symphony orchestras were severely lacking in patronage and financial resources; immigrant and Canadian artists, having settled down with their careers, were cut off from new developments in composition. Up to the middle of the 20th century, foreign composers usually outweighed Canadian ones as influences for young musicians. It was evident, however, that the Canadian sphere began to have an impact, especially in the popular sphere. As a result of adaptations to local conditions and the creation of new songs, French and Irish Canadians swapped dance tunes; amateurs and professionals competed to write patriotic music in the 1860s-1920s; and Northern social dances were preferred over European forms.
